# Writing Style Guide

This guide governs the prose in `_writings/` and, where applicable, other prose pages on the site (the Writings landing page, Mentorship, About). It exists so that every piece sounds like the same person: plain, formal, and direct. The guide follows its own rules.

## Voice and Register

- Write in the first person. Share what worked for you, not universal truths.
- Use simple formal academic wording. Formal means precise and unhurried; it does not mean stiff or ceremonial.
- Prefer the direct phrase over the hedged one: "I think" rather than "One might consider"; "email me" rather than "feel free to reach out".
- State uncertainty plainly. If a policy may have changed or a memory is fuzzy, say so in one clause and move on.

## Sentence Structure

- Mix short and long sentences. A short sentence after a long one gives the paragraph rhythm and marks what matters.
- Do not use em-dashes. Where an em-dash is tempting, use a comma, a colon, a period, or parentheses instead.
- Do not use apostrophes for contractions. Write "do not", "it is", "you are". (Possessives such as "the examiner's report" are correct and required.)
- Keep paragraphs to three or four sentences. Break up anything that looks like a wall of text with a heading, a list, or a shorter paragraph.

## Emphasis

- **Bold** marks the few load-bearing facts a skimming reader must not miss: deadlines, hard rules, numbers. Use it sparingly; a page with many bold phrases has none.
- *Italics* mark a word you would stress when speaking, or a term used as a term. One word or a short phrase, never a sentence.
- Never combine bold and italics. Never use emphasis as decoration.

## What Not to Do

- Do not use stilted or ceremonial phrasing. "One might consider contacting" becomes "I suggest emailing".
- Do not explain obvious things. Trust the reader's intelligence; define only what a new graduate student would genuinely not know.
- Do not preach. Present personal experience and verified facts; let the reader draw the conclusion.
- Do not hide uncertainty behind confident prose. "I believe this is still true, but confirm with the office" is better than a wrong certainty.
- Do not write walls of text. Use headings, lists, tables, and callouts to give the eye somewhere to land.
- Do not give generic advice. "Start early" is filler; "start the examiner conversation in July because the form needs eight weeks" is a sentence worth reading.

## Rewriting Examples

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| One might consider reaching out to the graduate advisor. | I suggest emailing the graduate advisor. |
| Booking opens now — do not wait for the report. | Booking opens now. Do not wait for the report. |
| You're not supposed to know who the examiner is. | You are not supposed to know who the examiner is. |
| It is critically important to always remember that deadlines matter. | The deadline is firm: five business days before your target date. |

## House Conventions for Writings

- Prefer evergreen framing over timely framing; a piece should still be useful in three years.
- Process posts carry a "last verified" note and link to the authoritative source.
- Never include confidential material: examiner shortlists, committee deliberations, or personal contact details of staff.
